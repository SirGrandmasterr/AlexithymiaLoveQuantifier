1. Eros (Romantic, Passionate Love)

Eros is the "chemistry" operating system. It is heavily driven by physical attraction, aesthetics, and a desire for rapid, intense connection. It is what most movies depict as "falling in love."

    Core Motivation: Physical and emotional merging; intense fascination with the partner's physical being.

    How to Detect It (Behavioral Metrics):

        Proximity Seeking: You find yourself constantly wanting to close the physical distance between you two (e.g., sitting side-by-side rather than across a table).

        Aesthetic Fixation: You frequently notice and focus on their physical features.

        Rapid Escalation: You feel a drive to escalate the relationship quickly, sharing deep secrets or engaging physically early on.

        The "Spark": You experience a noticeable physiological response (elevated heart rate, nervous energy) when you see them.

2. Ludus (Playful, Flirtatious Love)

Ludus views love as a game to be played or a dance to be enjoyed, rather than a heavy, long-term commitment. It is about the fun of the interaction without the weight of obligation.

    Core Motivation: Entertainment, freedom, and enjoying the "chase."

    How to Detect It (Behavioral Metrics):

        Lighthearted Communication: Conversations heavily feature banter, teasing, and flirting rather than deep, emotionally vulnerable topics.

        Avoidance of "The Future": You (or they) actively change the subject or feel a spike of discomfort when asked to define the relationship or make plans months in advance.

        Multiple Outputs: You feel comfortable and perhaps prefer pursuing or entertaining multiple romantic interests simultaneously.

        Emotional Boundaries: You do not feel a strong need to integrate this person into your broader life (introducing them to family or close friends).

3. Storge (Unconditional, Familial Love)

Storge is the "slow burn" operating system. It is love that grows gradually out of a foundation of deep friendship, shared values, and mutual trust. There is often no distinct moment of "falling" in love; it just becomes a fact over time.

    Core Motivation: Companionship, stability, and psychological comfort.

    How to Detect It (Behavioral Metrics):

        High Comfort Level: You feel entirely yourself around them. You do not feel the need to "perform" or hide your flaws.

        Shared Values Over Aesthetics: Your connection is built on shared interests, similar life goals, or intellectual alignment rather than physical chemistry.

        Slow Progression: Physical intimacy or romantic declarations happened significantly later in the relationship, feeling like a natural evolution of a friendship.

        Crisis Stability: In times of high stress, your first instinct is to lean on them for practical support and advice.

4. Pragma (Enduring, Logical Love)

Pragma is the pragmatic, checklist-driven operating system. It is a highly cognitive approach to love where a partner is evaluated based on their practical compatibility for a successful life, family, or partnership.

    Core Motivation: Long-term compatibility, practical success, and life alignment.

    How to Detect It (Behavioral Metrics):

        Checklist Evaluation: You mentally (or literally) evaluate them against a set of criteria: financial stability, career trajectory, parenting potential, or lifestyle habits.

        Rational Vetoes: You have actively walked away from someone you found highly attractive or fun because they did not meet your logical criteria for a long-term partner.

        Logistical Harmony: The relationship is characterized by smooth planning, shared financial goals, and efficient division of labor.

        Head Over Heart: Decisions about the relationship are made based on what makes logical sense rather than emotional impulses.

5. Mania (Obsessive, Intense Love)

Mania is an unstable, highly volatile operating system. It usually arises from low self-esteem or a fear of abandonment, leading to a desperate need for the partner's constant reassurance and attention.

    Core Motivation: Alleviating anxiety through complete possession and reassurance from the partner.

    How to Detect It (Behavioral Metrics):

        Metric of Response: You experience genuine distress, anxiety, or anger if they do not reply to a message within a specific timeframe.

        Extreme Jealousy: You feel highly threatened by their external friendships or independent activities.

        Emotional Rollercoaster: Your mood for the entire day is dictated entirely by how well your interactions with this person are going.

        Hyper-Vigilance: You frequently monitor their social media or whereabouts to ensure they are not abandoning you.

6. Agape (Selfless, Universal Love)

Agape is the altruistic operating system. It is an entirely selfless love where the well-being and happiness of the partner are prioritized over your own, without any expectation of reward or reciprocation.

    Core Motivation: The unconditional care, nurturing, and betterment of the other person.

    How to Detect It (Behavioral Metrics):

        Willing Sacrifice: You consistently give up your own resources (time, money, comfort) to improve their situation, and you do not harbor resentment for it.

        Forgiveness: You have a high capacity to forgive their mistakes or flaws because you view them with deep empathy.

        Zero Keeping Score: You do not keep a mental tally of "who owes who" favors or effort in the relationship.

        Prioritizing Their Joy: You feel genuine satisfaction simply from seeing them happy, even if you did not directly cause it or benefit from it.

7. Selflessness (Complete Lack of Ego)

Note: In traditional psychological models, this overlaps almost completely with "Agape." However, since your image separates it as "Complete lack of ego," we can define it as the absolute extreme end of the Agape spectrum.

    Core Motivation: Total removal of the "self" from the equation of the relationship.

    How to Detect It (Behavioral Metrics):

        Absence of Personal Demands: You do not enforce your own boundaries or needs if they conflict even slightly with the other person's.

        Identity Merging: You evaluate situations entirely through the lens of "what is best for them," completely omitting "what is best for me."
